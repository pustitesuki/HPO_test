Introduction

Payers TPAs IROs PBMs MCOs

If you run utilization review at a payer, TPA, IRO, PBM, or managed care organization, you are about to be sold a lot of AI. Most of it will demo well. Some of it will pilot well. Much of it will struggle the first time a determination is challenged, an auditor asks how the system moved from documentation to denial, or a physician reviewer overrides the AI and you need to explain why.

Foundation models are extraordinarily capable, and they will become part of UR workflows. The question is not whether to use AI. It is which architecture you can defend eighteen months from now.

The difference between AI you can deploy in regulated decision work and AI you cannot is rarely visible in the demo. It shows up later — when the system has to be defended, debugged, or improved.

Three questions cut through the marketing faster than any feature checklist. They map to the three disciplines any AI in regulated decision work has to get right. We call them the Control, Evaluate, Improve framework, and it is the operating discipline of GTS COMAND.

Question 01 — Control
Where does the human approval gate sit?

Any AI-generated determination that leaves the system should require explicit human authorization — before it goes out, not after. The approval gate is not a convenience feature.

The key follow-up question:
What happens when a queued determination hits a statutory deadline with no reviewer action taken?
Does the system escalate automatically?
Is external release blocked?
Is the missed action recorded in the audit trail?
Who gets notified, and how quickly?

The right answer is that any AI-generated determination that leaves the system does so only after an explicit authorization step appropriate to the decision type, risk level, and governing rules. For adverse or complex determinations, that means affirmative approval by the credentialed reviewer, with an immutable record of that approval.

Diagram · Approval Gate Flow 800 × 260 · AI draft → reviewer gate → authorized release (or escalation)
Red Flag Answer
"The AI generates the determination and your reviewer can review it." That is not a control — that is a hope that your reviewer is paying attention under production volume. Vigilance does not scale, and any architecture that depends on it is a compliance risk waiting to happen.

A useful follow-up: ask the vendor what happens when a queued determination approaches a statutory or contractual deadline without reviewer action. Does the system escalate to the right human, lock external release, preserve the audit trail, and record the missed or late action? Or does it silently release the determination because the clock ran out?

The correct system does not silently release anything. It escalates, records the event, preserves the audit trail, and prevents external release unless the required authorization occurs. Every event — action or inaction — is captured with a timestamp and actor identity.

The approval gate is where the management of an AI agent actually begins. Everything upstream is generation. Everything downstream is accountability. The gate is the handoff between the two.
Control Principle · GTS COMAND

You cannot manage what you cannot control. The approval gate is not a UX choice — it is the boundary at which the system's accountability starts.

Question 02 — Evaluate
Is your evaluator deterministic?

This is the question that separates serious architectures from impressive demos. A probabilistic system evaluated by another probabilistic model gives you correlated probability — not correctness.

Accepted guideline sources:
MTUS
ODG
ACOEM
National Coverage Determinations

This is the question that separates serious architectures from impressive demos.

If the answer is some variant of "we use another AI model to score the first model's output," you are looking at a probabilistic system evaluated by another probabilistic system. The two share training distributions, biases, and blind spots. When they agree, you have correlated probability. You do not have correctness.

Model-based scoring can be useful as a secondary QA signal, but it should not be the primary correctness mechanism for a regulated determination. A second model may help flag issues, but it cannot substitute for an explicit guideline representation with criteria, satisfaction logic, and source-linked evidence.

Architecture Principle · GTS CORE
Foundation Model →
Does the language work: reads clinical documentation, extracts facts, structures findings for the adjudication layer.
Ontology + Rules Engine →
Does the adjudication work: applies structured guideline criteria, runs explicit satisfaction logic, produces traceable verdicts.
Evaluation →
Runs against the rules engine — not against another model. The reference is deterministic, not probabilistic.
Diagram · Probabilistic vs. Deterministic Evaluation 800 × 380 · Model-on-model scoring vs. rules engine — correlated probability vs. correctness

A defensible answer connects evaluation to a deterministic source: a structured representation of the applied guideline — MTUS, ODG, ACOEM, the relevant National Coverage Determination — with explicit criteria and explicit satisfaction logic. The system does not just say "this met medical necessity." It says which criterion was satisfied, what evidence satisfied it, and how that evidence was extracted from the clinical record.

For organizations operating under accreditation or delegated-review obligations, the question is not merely whether the output looks reasonable, but whether the clinical review criteria and decision logic can be shown.

Red Flag Answer
"We have proprietary scoring." If the vendor cannot articulate criteria, satisfaction logic, and source linkage in specific terms, the evaluation layer is a black box. Black-box evaluation layers are difficult to defend under regulatory scrutiny. "Proprietary" is not a methodology.
You cannot improve what you cannot measure. Evaluation against a deterministic reference — not against another model — is the precondition for everything that comes next.

Question 03 — Improve
When the AI is wrong, what exactly changes?

Every system is wrong some of the time. The question is whether errors are localized or diffuse — and whether you can prove, twelve months later, which rules produced a given determination.

Layered remediation:
Extraction error → fix in extraction layer
Misapplied guideline → fix in guideline representation
New evidence → add to relevant criterion
Each fix targeted, traceable, isolated

Every system is wrong some of the time. The question is what happens next.

A weak answer: "We retrain the model." Retraining can be versioned, but it is a blunt remediation path. It changes behavior diffusely, requires broad regression testing, and makes it difficult to prove that a targeted guideline correction did not affect unrelated decisions. In a regulated environment, diffuse change is a version control problem you cannot win.

A good answer: errors are localized. A misextracted clinical fact is fixed in the extraction layer. A misapplied guideline is fixed in the structured guideline representation. A new piece of evidence is added to the relevant criterion. Each fix is targeted, traceable, and does not regress unrelated determinations.

Diagram · Layered Remediation Architecture 800 × 440 · Extraction / guideline representation / evidence criteria — targeted fixes, no diffuse regression
Can you replay a determination from twelve months ago against the exact rule set, evidence state, extraction output, model version, and reviewer action history that existed at the time — and produce an auditable record of why that determination was reached?
The Temporal Versioning Test

If the answer is no, the system cannot demonstrate why a determination from twelve months ago was reached on the logic that was then in effect. If the answer is yes, you have temporal versioning, and you can defend any prior decision on the rules and the actions that produced it.

This is the difference between a system that improves over time and a system that drifts. In a regulated environment, drift is a liability you cannot manage.

In Practice
What to expect in a real procurement conversation

Run these three questions and the field thins quickly. Vendors who pass all three are not the ones with the loudest marketing — they are the ones who have made the right architectural commitments.

Pattern recognition:
Talks around Q2 → probabilistic-on-probabilistic
Reframes Q1 as "trust your reviewers" → no real control plane
Treats Q3 as a roadmap item → retraining loop

Run these three questions in a real procurement conversation and the field thins quickly. Vendors selling probabilistic-on-probabilistic architectures will talk around the second question. Vendors without a real control plane will reframe the first question as "trust your reviewers." Vendors built on retraining loops will treat the third question as a roadmap item.

The vendors who pass all three are not the ones with the loudest marketing. They are the ones who have made architectural commitments that match the standard the work actually requires.

Key Insight
These questions are not anti-AI. They are pro-deployment. The more consequential the workflow, the more important it becomes to separate language generation from authorization, evaluation, and remediation. Foundation models should make reviewers faster and better informed. They should not be the unchecked source of regulated determinations.
